Bacterial genome sequencing is now routine in many clinical
settings. To make the best use of the resulting wealth of sequence
data, it needs to be correctly classified. However, the vast majority
of bacterial strains are only classified down to the species rank,
which in many cases comprises various commensal and pathogenic
strains.

The classification of bacterial genomes below the species rank is the
topic of our forthcoming manuscript entitled \textit{Classifying
  Bacterial Genomes Below the Species Rank}. In this accompanying
tutorial we also show how to detect the genomes of a particular target
organism, \emph{Escherichia coli} serotype O111:H8. Serotype O111:H8
belongs to the ``Big Six'' of food-borne pathogenic \emph{E. coli}
that are associated world-wide with disease outbreaks ranging from
gastroenteritis and bloody diarrhea to potentially fatal
complications, such as acute encephalopathy. One possible use of
genome classification below is to extract diagnostic markers from the
genomes, and we show how to do that for O111:H8.

Since \emph{E. coli} is one of the most intensively sequenced
bacterial taxa, we shall analyze over 7,000 genomes, which takes
perhaps an hour. And while this may seem quick when analyzing all
\emph{E. coli} genomes, any computational workflow with this kind of
run time is difficult to construct, amend, and maintain.

We therefore begin this tutorial in Chapter~\ref{ch:p} with a pilot
study on how to construct a phylogeny from a sample of 50 complete
\emph{E. coli} genomes that contains the 22 O111:H8 genomes that were
in Genbank on 17th June 2026 and 28 closely related genomes.  In
Chapter~\ref{ch:q} we show how to query a precomputed phylogeny of
7,386 \emph{E. coli} genomes to identify all available genomes of
O111:H8.  This complete sample turns out to contain not only the
original 22 O111:H8, but an additional 25 genomes hitherto not
classified as O111:H8.

The individual computations necessary for this workflow take no more
than a few seconds on a consumer-grade laptop thus avoiding the
lengthy wait for the underlying distance computation. In
Chapter~\ref{ch:m}, finally, the 47 genomes of \emph{E. coli} O111:H8
identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:q} are used to construct diagnostic PCR
primers for that serotype.
